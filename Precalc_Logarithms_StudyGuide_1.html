Section 01

What a Logarithm Actually Is

A logarithm answers one question. To what power must I raise the base to land on this number? That is the entire concept. Everything downstream is bookkeeping.

$$\log_b x = y \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad b^y = x \qquad (b>0,\; b\neq 1,\; x>0)$$

The two equations carry identical information. The exponential form hides the exponent inside; the logarithmic form drags it out into the open and names it $y$. A log is an exponent. Say that out loud before the exam.

🧠 Memory Hook
The spiral read. $\log_b x = y$. Start at the base, swing up to the answer, the exponent drops out. Read it as a loop: "$b$ raised to $y$ gives $x$."

Visual image: Picture the base as a launchpad on the ground. The number $x$ is a target in the sky. The logarithm is the throttle setting (the exponent) that gets you exactly there. Big target, big throttle.

The Two Forms, Side by Side

Logarithmic FormExponential FormRead As
$\log_2 8 = 3$$2^3 = 8$2 to the 3rd is 8
$\log_5 25 = 2$$5^2 = 25$5 to the 2nd is 25
$\log_{10} 1000 = 3$$10^3 = 1000$10 cubed is 1000
$\log_3 \tfrac{1}{9} = -2$$3^{-2} = \tfrac{1}{9}$negative exponent, reciprocal

Evaluating Without a Calculator

Ask the conversion question. $\log_4 64 = ?$ becomes "$4$ to the what is $64$?" Since $4^3 = 64$, the answer is $3$. No calculator, just the inverse relationship working for you.

🔑 The Four Pillar Identities
Memorize these cold; they collapse half of every problem.
$\log_b 1 = 0$  (because $b^0 = 1$)
$\log_b b = 1$  (because $b^1 = b$)
$\log_b b^x = x$  (the log undoes the exponential)
$b^{\log_b x} = x$  (the exponential undoes the log)
⚠️ Watch Out
The argument must be positive. $\log_b(\text{negative})$ and $\log_b 0$ are undefined. There is no real exponent that turns a positive base into a negative number or into zero. This single fact generates every extraneous solution you will reject later.
⚡ Exam Hack
Stuck on a log? Rewrite it as an exponential immediately. $\log_x 81 = 4$ looks intimidating until it becomes $x^4 = 81$, so $x = 3$. The exponential form is almost always the easier room to work in.
Section 02

The Three Rules

Logs convert one tier of operation down. Multiplication becomes addition. Division becomes subtraction. Exponentiation becomes multiplication. That downshift is the whole power of the tool; it is why slide rules and pre-calculator computation ran on logs for three centuries.

RuleFormWhat Happens
Product$\log_b(MN) = \log_b M + \log_b N$Multiply inside → add outside
Quotient$\log_b\!\left(\tfrac{M}{N}\right) = \log_b M - \log_b N$Divide inside → subtract outside
Power$\log_b(M^p) = p\log_b M$Exponent inside → drops to the front
🧠 Memory Hook
Rhyme: "Times turns to plus, divide turns to minus, the power comes down to the front of the line-up."

The downshift acronym — PAD: Product → Add, Divide → subtract. And the Power rule is the diva; the exponent demands the spotlight, so it walks to the front.

Absurd story: Picture two numbers getting married inside the log (multiplication). The log is the officiant who refuses to let them stay joined; it splits them into two separate logs holding hands with a plus sign. Divorce (division) splits them with a minus.

Why the Product Rule Works (one line)

Let $\log_b M = x$ and $\log_b N = y$. Then $M = b^x$ and $N = b^y$, so $MN = b^x b^y = b^{x+y}$. Convert back: $\log_b(MN) = x + y = \log_b M + \log_b N$. The exponent law $b^x b^y = b^{x+y}$ is the product rule wearing a different hat.

⚠️ The Errors That Sink People
These are not real rules. Burn them as forbidden.

1. $\log_b(M+N) \neq \log_b M + \log_b N$. The product rule needs a product inside, never a sum. There is no rule for the log of a sum. None.

2. $\log_b\!\left(\tfrac{M}{N}\right) \neq \dfrac{\log_b M}{\log_b N}$. The quotient rule gives a difference. A quotient of two logs is change of base, a different animal entirely.

3. $(\log_b M)^p \neq p\log_b M$. The power rule requires the exponent on the argument inside, not on the whole log. $(\log_b M)^2$ stays put.

Worked: Product Rule

Expand $\log_2(8x)$. The inside is a product, so split it: $\log_2 8 + \log_2 x$. Then $\log_2 8 = 3$, giving $3 + \log_2 x$. Two moves: split, then evaluate the piece you can.

Worked: Power Rule

Evaluate $\log_5 25^3$. Drop the exponent: $3\log_5 25$. Then $\log_5 25 = 2$, so $3 \cdot 2 = 6$. Check against the definition: $25^3 = 5^6$, and indeed $\log_5(5^6) = 6$. Confirmed.

⚡ Exam Hack
A radical is a fractional exponent in disguise. $\log_b \sqrt{x} = \log_b x^{1/2} = \tfrac{1}{2}\log_b x$. The instant you see a root inside a log, rewrite it as a power so the power rule can act on it.
Section 03

Expanding & Condensing

Two directions, same three rules. Expanding breaks one log into a sum and difference of simpler logs. Condensing collapses a string of logs back into one. Free-response problems love both; the partial credit lives in the intermediate steps.

Expanding: Outside In

1
Handle the outermost operation first. If the whole inside is a fraction, apply the quotient rule before anything else.
2
Apply the product rule to any products in the numerator or denominator.
3
Apply the power rule to bring every exponent (including roots written as fractional powers) to the front.
4
Evaluate any log that resolves to a clean number.

Worked Expansion

Expand $\log_b\!\dfrac{x^3\sqrt{y}}{z^2}$.

$$\log_b\frac{x^3\sqrt{y}}{z^2} = \log_b(x^3\sqrt{y}) - \log_b(z^2)$$$$= \log_b x^3 + \log_b y^{1/2} - \log_b z^2$$$$= 3\log_b x + \tfrac{1}{2}\log_b y - 2\log_b z$$

Notice the order: quotient first (the fraction bar is the outermost cut), then product on the numerator, then power on each piece. Reverse that order and the signs go feral.

⚠️ Watch Out — The Sign Trap
Everything in the denominator gets subtracted, and a power there still subtracts. $\log_b \dfrac{1}{x^4} = \log_b 1 - \log_b x^4 = 0 - 4\log_b x = -4\log_b x$. The negative sign and the coefficient both have to survive the trip.

Condensing: The Reverse Gear

To condense, every log must first have a coefficient of $1$. Use the power rule backwards to push coefficients up as exponents, then merge with product and quotient rules.

Worked Condensation

Condense $2\log_b x + \tfrac{1}{2}\log_b y - 3\log_b z$ into a single logarithm.

$$2\log_b x + \tfrac{1}{2}\log_b y - 3\log_b z = \log_b x^2 + \log_b y^{1/2} - \log_b z^3$$$$= \log_b\!\left(x^2 y^{1/2}\right) - \log_b z^3 = \log_b\frac{x^2\sqrt{y}}{z^3}$$
⚡ Exam Hack
A leading coefficient on a log must become an exponent before you can merge. You cannot combine $2\log x + \log y$ as $\log(2xy)$. The $2$ is a power, not a multiplier: it is $\log(x^2 y)$. This is the single most common condensing error.
🔑 Direction Check
Plus signs between logs condense to multiplication. Minus signs condense to division. Coefficients become exponents. Run it the other way for expansion. Same machinery, opposite throttle.
Section 04

Change of Base

Your calculator speaks two log dialects: base 10 and base $e$. Everything else needs translation. The change-of-base formula is that translator.

$$\log_b x = \frac{\log_a x}{\log_a b}$$

Pick any new base $a$ you can actually compute. In practice $a = 10$ or $a = e$, so:

$$\log_b x = \frac{\log x}{\log b} = \frac{\ln x}{\ln b}$$
🧠 Memory Hook
"New base in the basement." The original base $b$ moves downstairs into the denominator. The argument $x$ stays up top. Whatever base you switch to, both new logs share it.

Watch the trap: a quotient of two logs is change of base. It is NOT the quotient rule. The quotient rule produces a difference of logs; change of base produces a fraction of logs. Same words, opposite structures.

Worked: Evaluate a Strange Base

Compute $\log_3 50$. Base 3 is not on the calculator, so switch to natural log.

$$\log_3 50 = \frac{\ln 50}{\ln 3} = \frac{3.912}{1.099} \approx 3.561$$

Sanity check: $3^3 = 27$ and $3^4 = 81$, so $\log_3 50$ should sit between $3$ and $4$, closer to $3.5$. The answer $3.561$ lands exactly where geometry says it should.

⚡ Exam Hack
Either base works and both give the same number. $\dfrac{\log 50}{\log 3} = \dfrac{1.699}{0.477} \approx 3.561$ matches the natural-log version. Use whichever button you trust. On a free-response, write the formula with the substituted values before you reach for the decimal; that line is worth points even if the arithmetic slips.
🔑 Why It Holds
Let $y = \log_b x$, so $b^y = x$. Take $\ln$ of both sides: $\ln(b^y) = \ln x$, then power rule gives $y\ln b = \ln x$, so $y = \dfrac{\ln x}{\ln b}$. The formula is just the power rule applied to the definition.
Section 05

Common Log, Natural Log & the Number e

Common Log

$\log x$ means $\log_{10} x$. The base 10 is silent. Built for scientific notation, decibels, pH, the Richter scale.

Natural Log

$\ln x$ means $\log_e x$. The base is Euler's number $e \approx 2.71828$. The native language of continuous growth and calculus.

The Number e

$e$ is the limit of $\left(1 + \tfrac{1}{n}\right)^n$ as $n$ grows without bound. It is the base where the rate of growth equals the current amount; continuous, self-feeding, frictionless growth. That property is why $e$ runs every continuous-compounding and natural-decay model you will meet.

🔑 The Natural-Log Identities
Same four pillars, base $e$.
$\ln 1 = 0$  ·  $\ln e = 1$  ·  $\ln e^x = x$  ·  $e^{\ln x} = x$
These cancellations are the engine of solving exponential equations. When you take $\ln$ of $e^{(\text{stuff})}$, the stuff falls straight out.
🧠 Memory Hook
"$\ln$ and $e$ are undo buttons for each other." Whatever $e$ raises up, $\ln$ pulls back down. $\ln(e^7) = 7$. $e^{\ln 7} = 7$. They cancel like a sneeze and a tissue.

Notation discipline: $\log$ with no base is 10. $\ln$ is always $e$. Mixing them is the silent error that ruins an otherwise perfect solve.

The Three Bases at a Glance

NotationBaseWhere It Lives
$\log_b x$any $b$General theory; needs change of base to compute
$\log x$$10$pH, decibels, Richter, orders of magnitude
$\ln x$$e$Continuous growth/decay, half-life, calculus
⚠️ Watch Out
$\ln x$ is not $\dfrac{1}{\log x}$, and $\log x$ is not $\dfrac{\log_{10}}{x}$. They are different functions with different bases, related only through change of base: $\ln x = \dfrac{\log x}{\log e} \approx 2.303\log x$. The constant $2.303$ is $\ln 10$; it shows up everywhere that chemistry converts between the two.
Section 06

Graphing Logarithmic Functions

The log graph is the exponential graph viewed in a mirror. Reflect $y = b^x$ across the line $y = x$ and you get $y = \log_b x$. Every feature of one maps to a swapped feature of the other, because they are inverse functions.

Feature$y = b^x$ (exponential)$y = \log_b x$ (logarithm)
Domain$(-\infty, \infty)$$(0, \infty)$
Range$(0, \infty)$$(-\infty, \infty)$
AsymptoteHorizontal: $y = 0$Vertical: $x = 0$
Key point$(0, 1)$$(1, 0)$
Second point$(1, b)$$(b, 1)$
🔑 The Parent Graph (b > 1)
$y = \log_b x$ slides up the page slowly and forever. It crosses the x-axis at $(1, 0)$, climbs through $(b, 1)$, and hugs the y-axis on the left without ever touching it. Domain is strictly positive; range is all reals. If $0 < b < 1$, the curve flips to decreasing.
🧠 Memory Hook
"Logs are shy on the left." The curve never crosses into negative $x$; the vertical asymptote at $x = 0$ is a wall. Picture the graph pressing against an invisible glass door at the y-axis, sliding up it but never through it.

Transformations (same table as Ch 1–3)

Logs transform by the identical shift/stretch/reflect rules you already drilled. The one extra thing to track: horizontal shifts move the vertical asymptote, which redefines the domain.

FunctionEffectAsymptote & Domain
$\log_b(x-h)$Right $h$Asymptote $x=h$; domain $(h,\infty)$
$\log_b(x+h)$Left $h$Asymptote $x=-h$; domain $(-h,\infty)$
$\log_b(x)+k$Up $k$Asymptote stays $x=0$
$-\log_b(x)$Reflect over x-axisAsymptote stays $x=0$
$\log_b(-x)$Reflect over y-axisAsymptote $x=0$; domain $(-\infty,0)$
⚡ Exam Hack
To find the domain of any log, set the argument greater than zero and solve. For $y = \log_2(x - 3)$: require $x - 3 > 0$, so $x > 3$, domain $(3, \infty)$, asymptote $x = 3$. The domain question and the asymptote question have the same answer.
⚠️ Watch Out
The horizontal shift inside is still counterintuitive, exactly like in Chapter 3. $\log_b(x - 3)$ moves the graph and its asymptote to the right, not the left. Minus inside goes right.
Section 07

Solving Logarithmic & Exponential Equations

Type A — Variable in the Exponent

1
Isolate the exponential expression on one side.
2
Take a log of both sides (use $\ln$ if the base is $e$, otherwise any base).
3
Use the power rule to bring the variable exponent down in front.
4
Solve the resulting linear equation; evaluate with change of base if needed.

Worked: $5^x = 17$

$$5^x = 17 \;\Rightarrow\; \ln(5^x) = \ln 17 \;\Rightarrow\; x\ln 5 = \ln 17$$$$x = \frac{\ln 17}{\ln 5} = \frac{2.833}{1.609} \approx 1.760$$

Check: $5^{1.76} \approx 17$. And $5^1 = 5$, $5^2 = 25$, so $x$ between $1$ and $2$ is structurally correct.

Worked: $e^{2x} = 40$ (base e shortcut)

When the base is $e$, take $\ln$ and the left side collapses instantly: $\ln(e^{2x}) = 2x$.

$$e^{2x} = 40 \;\Rightarrow\; 2x = \ln 40 \;\Rightarrow\; x = \frac{\ln 40}{2} = \frac{3.689}{2} \approx 1.844$$

Type B — Variable Inside a Log

1
Condense to a single logarithm if more than one log appears.
2
Rewrite in exponential form (or, if both sides are single logs of the same base, set the arguments equal).
3
Solve the resulting algebraic equation.
4
Check every solution against the domain. Reject any answer that makes an argument zero or negative.

Worked: $\log_2 x + \log_2(x-2) = 3$

$$\log_2\big(x(x-2)\big) = 3 \;\Rightarrow\; x(x-2) = 2^3 = 8$$$$x^2 - 2x - 8 = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; (x-4)(x+2) = 0$$$$x = 4 \quad\text{or}\quad x = -2$$

Domain check. The equation needs $x > 0$ and $x - 2 > 0$, so $x > 2$. The candidate $x = 4$ survives. The candidate $x = -2$ is extraneous; it would feed a negative number into a log. Final answer: $x = 4$ only.

⚠️ Watch Out — Extraneous Solutions
Squaring and condensing can manufacture answers that the original equation forbids. A log equation is not finished when the algebra ends. It is finished when you have tested each root against the argument-positive requirement and crossed out the impostors. Skipping this step is the most penalized mistake in the unit.
🧠 Memory Hook
Absurd story: Every log is a bouncer at a club, and the argument has to be positive to get past the door. You solve the equation and walk your answers up to the entrance. The bouncer checks IDs. Negative arguments get tossed onto the curb, no matter how charming the algebra was. Only the positives dance.
⚡ Exam Hack
If the equation is $\log_b A = \log_b B$ (single log, same base, both sides), skip the exponential step and set $A = B$ directly. The function is one-to-one, so equal logs force equal arguments. Still domain-check the result.
Section 08

Applications

Logs are the inverse key that unlocks any model where the unknown sits in an exponent. Money, populations, radioactive isotopes, sound, earthquakes, acidity. Same algebra, different costume.

Compound & Continuous Interest

$$A = P\left(1 + \frac{r}{n}\right)^{nt} \qquad\text{(compounded } n \text{ times/year)}$$$$A = Pe^{rt} \qquad\text{(compounded continuously)}$$

Worked. How long to double \$1000 at 5% compounded continuously?

$$2000 = 1000\,e^{0.05t} \;\Rightarrow\; 2 = e^{0.05t} \;\Rightarrow\; \ln 2 = 0.05t$$$$t = \frac{\ln 2}{0.05} = \frac{0.693}{0.05} \approx 13.86 \text{ years}$$

The principal vanished from the equation. Doubling time never depends on how much you started with; it depends only on the rate. The "Rule of 70" is this exact formula approximated: $70/5 = 14$, within a rounding hair of $13.86$.

Exponential Growth & Decay

$$A = A_0 e^{kt} \qquad (k>0 \text{ growth},\; k<0 \text{ decay})$$

Half-life worked. A sample with half-life $h$ decays by $A = A_0 e^{kt}$ where $k = -\dfrac{\ln 2}{h}$. The negative sign and the $\ln 2$ both come straight from solving $\tfrac{1}{2}A_0 = A_0 e^{kh}$ for $k$.

Logarithmic Scales

ScaleFormulaWhat Moving "+1" Means
pH$\text{pH} = -\log[\text{H}^+]$10× less acidic per unit up
Decibels$\beta = 10\log\!\frac{I}{I_0}$10× more intense per +10 dB
Richter$M = \log\!\frac{A}{A_0}$10× larger amplitude per unit up

pH worked. A solution with $[\text{H}^+] = 10^{-4}$ has $\text{pH} = -\log(10^{-4}) = -(-4) = 4$. Acidic. The minus sign in the formula exists precisely so that small concentrations produce friendly positive pH numbers.

🔑 The Unifying Idea
Every log scale compresses multiplicative jumps into additive steps. A Richter 7 is not "a bit worse" than a Richter 6; it is ten times the amplitude. Logs turn the runaway exponential reality into a number line humans can read. That compression is the entire reason these scales exist.
⚡ Exam Hack
Application problems almost always reduce to: plug in knowns, isolate the exponential, take a log, solve. Spot the structure first. If the unknown is trapped in an exponent, the log is your crowbar. Write the model equation before touching numbers; that line earns setup credit independent of the final arithmetic.

Test Yourself

Selected Practice Problems

Q1. Evaluate $\log_4 64$ without a calculator.
"4 to the what is 64?" $4^3 = 64$, so the answer is 3.
Q2. Solve $\log_x 81 = 4$.
Exponential form: $x^4 = 81 \to x = 3$ (base must be positive, so reject $-3$). $x = 3$.
Q3. Expand $\log_2(8x)$.
$\log_2 8 + \log_2 x = 3 + \log_2 x$. $3+\log_2 x$.
Q4. Expand $\log_b\dfrac{x^3\sqrt{y}}{z^2}$.
Quotient, then product, then power: $3\log_b x + \frac{1}{2}\log_b y - 2\log_b z$.
Q5. Condense $2\log_b x + \frac{1}{2}\log_b y - 3\log_b z$.
Coefficients become exponents, plus → times, minus → divide: $\log_b\dfrac{x^2\sqrt{y}}{z^3}$.
Q6. Why is $\log_b(M+N) = \log_b M + \log_b N$ wrong?
The product rule needs a product inside, not a sum. There is no rule for the log of a sum; it cannot be split.
Q7. Evaluate $\log_3 50$ to three decimals.
$\frac{\ln 50}{\ln 3} = \frac{3.912}{1.099} \approx$ 3.561 (between $\log_3 27=3$ and $\log_3 81=4$, as expected).
Q8. Solve $5^x = 17$.
$x\ln 5 = \ln 17 \to x = \frac{\ln 17}{\ln 5} \approx$ 1.760.
Q9. Solve $e^{2x} = 40$.
$2x = \ln 40 \to x = \frac{\ln 40}{2} \approx$ 1.844.
Q10. Solve $\log_2 x + \log_2(x-2) = 3$.
$x(x-2)=8 \to x^2-2x-8=0 \to (x-4)(x+2)=0$. Domain needs $x>2$, so reject $-2$. $x=4$.
Q11. Find the domain of $y = \log_2(x-3)$.
Argument $>0$: $x-3>0 \to x>3$. Domain $(3,\infty)$; asymptote $x=3$.
Q12. Doubling time for 5% continuous interest?
$2=e^{0.05t} \to t=\frac{\ln 2}{0.05} \approx$ 13.86 years (principal-independent).
Q13. Find the pH when $[\text{H}^+] = 10^{-4}$.
$\text{pH} = -\log(10^{-4}) = -(-4) =$ 4.
Q14. Simplify $e^{\ln 7}$ and $\ln(e^5)$.
Inverse cancellations: $e^{\ln 7} = $ 7, and $\ln(e^5) = $ 5.

Final 5-Minute Review

The whole unit hangs on one sentence: a logarithm is an exponent. Write $\log_b x = y$ and you have written $b^y = x$ in disguise. From there, the three rules are exponent laws read backwards; multiplication downshifts to addition, division to subtraction, powers to multiplication. Guard the three forbidden moves: no splitting the log of a sum, no confusing a quotient of logs with the quotient rule, no pulling an outer power inside. Change of base sends the original base to the basement so your calculator can finish the job. Graphs are the exponential in a mirror, shy on the left, walled off at $x=0$. Equations come in two flavors: variable in the exponent, take a log; variable in the argument, go exponential and then check for impostors. Applications are all the same crowbar; when the unknown hides in an exponent, the log pries it loose. See the inverse relationship underneath every problem and the unit stops being a list of tricks. It becomes one idea, told eight ways.
